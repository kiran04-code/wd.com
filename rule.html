Paralympics


"The Paralympics show that strength isn’t in the body, but in the will to overcome. Every challenge is an opportunity to prove your true power."

Overview

The Paralympic Games are a major international sporting event that features athletes with physical, visual, and intellectual disabilities. These games parallel the Olympic Games and are held every four years, following the Olympics, at both the summer and winter editions. They are governed by the International Paralympic Committee (IPC) and are a celebration of athletic excellence, perseverance, and inclusion.

Events and Disciplines

The Paralympic Games feature a diverse range of events tailored for athletes with physical, visual, and intellectual disabilities. Summer Paralympic sports include archery, athletics (track and field), badminton, boccia, cycling (road and track), equestrian, goalball, judo, powerlifting, rowing, shooting, sitting volleyball, swimming, table tennis, taekwondo, wheelchair basketball, wheelchair fencing, wheelchair rugby, and wheelchair tennis. Each sport has specific disciplines, such as sprints, relays, and field events in athletics, freestyle and medley events in swimming, or precision-based sports like archery and shooting. Winter Paralympic sports include alpine skiing, cross-country skiing, biathlon, para ice hockey, snowboarding, and wheelchair curling, with athletes competing using adaptive equipment like sit-skis and sledges. These events are divided into classifications to ensure fair competition based on the type and degree of disability, showcasing incredible skill, resilience, and determination.

Rules & Equipment

The rules and equipment in the Paralympics are adapted to ensure fair competition and inclusivity for athletes with different disabilities. Each sport follows guidelines set by its governing body, with modifications to accommodate physical, visual, or intellectual impairments. For example, wheelchair sports like basketball and rugby require specially designed, lightweight wheelchairs for agility and speed, while goalball uses a ball with bells to aid visually impaired players. In archery and shooting, adaptive equipment like mouth triggers or harnesses can be used. Swimming allows for customized starting procedures, while tandem bikes are used for visually impaired athletes in cycling, with a sighted pilot. All sports use a classification system to group athletes based on the type and severity of their disabilities, ensuring a level playing field while maintaining the integrity and challenge of the competition.

Classification

Classification in the Paralympics is a system designed to ensure fair competition by grouping athletes based on the type and extent of their disabilities. There are three main categories: physical impairments (e.g., spinal cord injuries, limb deficiencies, or cerebral palsy), visual impairments (ranging from partial vision to complete blindness), and intellectual disabilities (affecting cognitive functions and decision-making). Each sport has its own classification criteria, as different abilities are required. For example, in athletics, athletes are classified into groups like T (track) or F (field), followed by a number indicating their level of impairment (e.g., T11 for visually impaired runners). Similarly, swimming uses the S, SB, and SM categories for different stroke events. These classifications are periodically reviewed to ensure fairness and accurate representation of the athletes' abilities while allowing them to compete on an equal playing field.

PCI

The Paralympic Committee of India is the apex governing body dedicated to promoting and developing Para Sport in the country. The Paralympic Committee of India spearheads the Paralympic Movement, organizes the Paralympic Games, and supports its members, empowering Para athletes to achieve excellence through dedicated training and opportunities.
